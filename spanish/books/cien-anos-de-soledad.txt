Cien Anos De Soledad ( Page 1 ) - Spanish
-------------------------------------------

Muchos años después, frente al pelotón de fusilamiento, el coronel Aureliano Buendía había 
de recordar aquella tarde remota en que su padre lo llevó a conocer el hielo. Macondo era 
entonces una aldea de veinte casas de barro y cañabrava construidas a la orilla de un río de 
aguas diáfanas que se precipitaban por un lecho de piedras pulidas, blancas y enormes como 
huevos prehistóricos. El mundo era tan reciente, que muchas cosas carecían de nombre, y para 
mencionarlas había que señalarías con el dedo. Todos los años, por el mes de marzo, una familia 
de gitanos desarrapados plantaba su carpa cerca de la aldea, y con un grande alboroto de pitos 
y timbales daban a conocer los nuevos inventos. Primero llevaron el imán. Un gitano corpulento, 
de barba montaraz y manos de gorrión, que se presentó con el nombre de Melquiades, hizo una 
truculenta demostración pública de lo que él mismo llamaba la octava maravilla de los sabios 
alquimistas de Macedonia. Fue de casa en casa arrastrando dos lingotes metálicos, y todo el 
mundo se espantó al ver que los calderos, las pailas, las tenazas y los anafes se caían de su 
sitio, y las maderas crujían por la desesperación de los clavos y los tornillos tratando de 
desenclavarse, y aun los objetos perdidos desde hacía mucho tiempo aparecían por donde más se 
les había buscado, y se arrastraban en desbandada turbulenta detrás de los fierros mágicos de 
Melquíades. «Las cosas, tienen vida propia —pregonaba el gitano con áspero acento—, todo es 
cuestión de despertarles el ánima.» José Arcadio Buendía, cuya desaforada imaginación iba siempre 
más lejos que el ingenio de la naturaleza, y aun más allá del milagro y la magia, pensó que era 
posible servirse de aquella invención inútil para desentrañar el oro de la tierra. Melquíades, 
que era un hombre honrado, le previno: «Para eso no sirve.» Pero José Arcadio Buendía no creía 
en aquel tiempo en la honradez de los gitanos, así que cambió su mulo y una partida de chivos 
por los dos lingotes imantados. Úrsula Iguarán, su mujer, que contaba con aquellos animales para 
ensanchar el desmedrado patrimonio doméstico, no consiguió disuadirlo. «Muy pronto ha de sobrarnos 
oro para empedrar la casa», replicó su marido. Durante varios meses se empeñó en demostrar el acierto 
de sus conjeturas. Exploró palmo a palmo la región, inclusive el fondo del río, arrastrando los dos 
lingotes de hierro y recitando en voz alta el conjuro de Melquíades. Lo único que logró desenterrar 
fue una armadura del siglo xv con todas sus partes soldadas por un cascote de óxido, cuyo interior 
tenía la resonancia hueca de un enorme calabazo lleno de piedras. Cuando José Arcadio Buendía y los 
cuatro hombres de su expedición lograron desarticular la armadura, encontraron dentro un esqueleto 
calcificado que llevaba colgado en el cuello un relicario de cobre con un rizo de mujer.

One Hundred Years Of Solitude ( Page 1 ) - English
---------------------------------------------------

Many years later, facing the firing squad, Colonel Aureliano Buendía had to remember that remote afternoon 
when his father took him to discover ice. Macondo was then a village of twenty mud and reed houses built on 
the banks of a river with clear waters that rushed through a bed of polished stones, white and enormous like 
prehistoric eggs. The world was so recent that many things lacked names, and to mention them you had to point 
your finger. Every year, during the month of March, a family of ragged gypsies would pitch their tent near 
the village, and with a great uproar of whistles and kettledrums they would announce their new inventions. 
First they brought the magnet. A corpulent gypsy, with a wild beard and sparrow-like hands, who introduced 
himself under the name of Melquiades, gave a gruesome public demonstration of what he himself called the 
eighth wonder of the wise alchemists of Macedonia. He went from house to house dragging two metal ingots, 
and everyone was frightened to see that the cauldrons, the pots, the tongs and the stoves were falling 
from their places, and the wood creaking from the desperation of the nails and screws trying to be unlocked, 
and even the objects lost for a long time appeared where they had been most sought, and were dragged in 
turbulent disarray behind the magical irons of Melquíades. "Things have a life of their own," the gypsy 
proclaimed with a harsh accent, "it's all a matter of awakening their soul." José Arcadio Buendía, whose 
wild imagination always went further than the ingenuity of nature, and even beyond miracle and magic, 
thought that it was possible to use that useless invention to unravel gold from the earth. Melquíades, who 
was an honest man, warned him: "It's not good for that." But José Arcadio Buendía did not believe in the 
honesty of gypsies at that time, so he exchanged his mule and a batch of goats for the two magnetized ingots. 
Úrsula Iguarán, his wife, who was counting on those animals to expand their diminished domestic assets, was 
unable to dissuade him. "Very soon we will have more gold to pave the house," her husband replied. For 
several months she insisted on proving the correctness of her conjectures. He explored the region inch by 
inch, including the bottom of the river, dragging the two iron ingots and reciting out loud the incantation 
of Melquiades. The only thing he managed to unearth was a 15th century suit of armor with all its parts 
welded together by a piece of rust, the interior of which had the hollow resonance of a huge gourd full 
of stones. When José Arcadio Buendía and the four men of his expedition managed to dismantle the armor, 
they found inside a calcified skeleton with a copper reliquary with a woman's curl hanging around its neck.

### Subject Pronouns with Verbs
#### Yo (I)
- **yo llevo** (I take) - Present tense
  - "lo llevó" (he took him) can be transformed into "yo llevo" (I take)
- **yo recuerdo** (I remember) - Present tense
  - "recordar" (to remember) can be used with "yo" as "yo recuerdo"

#### Tú (You)
- **tú llevas** (you take) - Present tense
  - "llevó" (took) can be transformed into "tú llevas" (you take)
- **tú recuerdas** (you remember) - Present tense
  - "recordar" (to remember) can be used with "tú" as "tú recuerdas"

#### Él/Ella/Usted (He/She/You formal)
- **él/ella/usted lleva** (he/she/you formal take) - Present tense
  - "lo llevó" (he took him)
- **él/ella/usted recuerda** (he/she/you formal remember) - Present tense
  - "recordar" (to remember) used as "él recuerda" (he remembers)

#### Nosotros/Nosotras (We)
- **nosotros llevamos** (we take) - Present tense
  - "llevó" (took) can be transformed into "nosotros llevamos" (we take)
- **nosotros recordamos** (we remember) - Present tense
  - "recordar" (to remember) can be used with "nosotros" as "nosotros recordamos"

#### Vosotros/Vosotras (You all - Spain)
- **vosotros lleváis** (you all take) - Present tense
  - "llevó" (took) can be transformed into "vosotros lleváis" (you all take)
- **vosotros recordáis** (you all remember) - Present tense
  - "recordar" (to remember) can be used with "vosotros" as "vosotros recordáis"

#### Ellos/Ellas/Ustedes (They/You all formal)
- **ellos/ellas/ustedes llevan** (they/you all formal take) - Present tense
  - "llevó" (took) can be transformed into "ellos llevan" (they take)
- **ellos/ellas/ustedes recuerdan** (they/you all formal remember) - Present tense
  - "recordar" (to remember) used as "ellos recuerdan" (they remember)

### Examples from the Paragraph
- **llevó** (took) - Past tense, 3rd person singular
  - "su padre lo llevó" (his father took him)

- **recordar** (remember) - Infinitive form, used in "había de recordar" (was to remember)
  - "había de recordar" (was to remember) - Past tense, 3rd person singular
  - Can be transformed to "yo recuerdo" (I remember), "tú recuerdas" (you remember), etc.

- **había** (had) - Past tense, 3rd person singular
  - "había de recordar" (was to remember)
